Voice Technique

How to Stop Your Voice From Shaking When the Stakes Are High

15 June 2026  ·  Alisa Amouage

The moment you care most about what you are about to say is often the moment your voice decides to betray you.

You have prepared. You know your material. And yet the second you stand up — or pick up the phone for that important call — your voice tightens, rises in pitch, and develops a tremor you have never noticed before.

I know this feeling intimately. Not just from coaching speakers, but from my own years on stage. The bigger the room, the bigger the occasion, the louder the internal noise. And here is what I have learned: a shaking voice is not a weakness. It is a physiological response. Understanding that changes everything.

What Is Actually Happening

When the stakes feel high, your nervous system activates a stress response. Cortisol and adrenaline flood your system. Your muscles tense — including the muscles around your larynx. Your breath becomes shallow, moving from your belly up into your chest. Less breath, more tension, and your vocal cords begin to vibrate unevenly.

The result: a voice that shakes, rises, or disappears entirely at the worst possible moment.

This is not a character flaw. It is your body trying to protect you from what it perceives as danger. The problem is that your body cannot tell the difference between a high-stakes presentation and an actual threat. It responds to both the same way.

The Technique That Changes It

The answer is not to "calm down" — that instruction never works and only adds pressure. The answer is to redirect the breath before you speak.

Here is what I do, and what I teach every client: before you say your first word, take one slow breath that expands your belly outward — not your chest upward. Hold it for two counts. Then release it slowly. Let it fall, do not push it out.

This single breath activates your parasympathetic nervous system. It signals to your body that you are safe. Within seconds, the tension in your larynx begins to ease.

Train It Before You Need It

The challenge is that this technique only works under pressure if you have practised it without pressure. Like any physical skill, it must become automatic before the stakes are high enough to override conscious thought.

My recommendation: every morning, before you speak your first sentence of the day — whether to a colleague, on a call, or to yourself — take that one belly breath first. Make it a ritual. Three weeks of this and your voice will know exactly what to do when it matters most.

Your voice is not failing you in those moments. It is asking for guidance. Give it one breath, and it will give you everything you need.

The 5-Minute Vocal Warm-Up I Do Before Every Performance

5 June 2026  ·  Alisa Amouage

People are often surprised when I tell them that the five minutes before I walk on stage look nothing like what they might expect.

No pacing. No last-minute rehearsals. No running through the setlist in my head. Just five deliberate minutes of physical preparation — the same routine, whether I am performing at a hotel gala for three hundred guests or a private dinner for twenty.

After fifteen years of performing, this is the routine I have refined down to what actually works.

Minute 1 — The Breath Reset

Before I touch my voice, I reset my breath. I stand with feet hip-width apart, place one hand on my belly, and breathe in slowly for four counts — letting my hand rise outward. I hold for two counts, then exhale for six. I do this three times. By the third breath, I can feel my shoulders drop and my jaw loosen. This is the foundation. Everything else builds on it.

Minute 2 — Lip Trills

I close my lips gently and blow air through them so they flutter — like the sound of a small motorboat. I run this trill up and down a gentle scale, five notes up and five notes down. This warms the lips, engages the breath support, and begins to loosen the vocal cords without straining them. If your lips do not trill easily at first, place your fingers lightly on your cheeks to support the muscles.

Minute 3 — Humming

With lips closed, I hum on a comfortable mid-range note and slide it slowly up and down — not forcing the high or low extremes, just exploring the middle of my range. I feel for the vibration in my chest first, then in my nose and cheeks. This activates resonance — the quality that gives a voice presence and warmth across a room.

Minute 4 — Articulation

I work through a sequence of consonant combinations: "puh-puh-puh", "tuh-tuh-tuh", "kuh-kuh-kuh" — fast, crisp, deliberate. Then: "red lorry, yellow lorry", said slowly at first, then at full speed. Crisp articulation is what allows your words to land clearly at the back of a large room without raising your volume.

Minute 5 — Siren and Settle

I finish with one slow siren — a sound that glides from the bottom of my range to the top and back down, like a signal moving away in the distance. Then I stop. One more belly breath. And I let the voice settle in silence for a moment before stepping on stage.

That silence at the end is not wasted time. It is when the body integrates everything and prepares to perform.

Five minutes. Every time. It has never let me down.

What Jazz Taught Me About Holding a Room's Attention

20 May 2026  ·  Alisa Amouage

I spent years trying to figure out what separates a performer who merely fills a room from one who genuinely holds it.

It is not the size of the voice. It is not technical perfection. And it is certainly not volume.

What I discovered — slowly, through hundreds of performances and the discipline of jazz — is that the real skill is listening.

Jazz Is a Conversation

Most people think of performing as something you do to an audience. You deliver. They receive. The energy flows one way.

Jazz taught me that this is wrong — or at least, incomplete.

In jazz, musicians listen to each other constantly. Every phrase I sing is a response to what the pianist just played. His next phrase responds to mine. The drummer listens to both of us and shifts accordingly. A jazz performance, at its best, is a live conversation between musicians — and when it is working, the audience becomes part of that conversation without even realising it.

What This Looks Like in Practice

When I walk onto a stage, the first thing I do is listen to the room before I sing a single note. I feel the energy — is the audience quiet and expectant? Restless? Distracted by noise from the bar? Warmed up and ready?

Then I adjust. Not drastically — but in the same way a jazz pianist might shift the tempo slightly to bring a wandering room back, I shift my energy, my pacing, my volume, my eye contact to meet the audience where they are.

A performer who ignores the audience's energy and delivers a fixed, rehearsed set will always feel like a presentation. A performer who listens and responds will always feel like an experience.

The Pause Is Part of the Performance

Jazz also taught me the power of silence. A well-placed pause — before a key line, after a phrase that needs to land, at the moment of highest tension — is not empty space. It is some of the most powerful real estate in a performance.

Silence invites the audience to lean in. It creates anticipation. And it signals confidence: only someone who is truly in control of a room is comfortable letting it be quiet.

Whether you are a singer, a speaker, a leader, or anyone who has to command attention — learn to pause. Learn to listen. Learn to respond to the room rather than simply perform at it.

That is what jazz taught me. And it has changed everything about how I communicate.

Why Live Music Changes the Energy in a Room

28 June 2026  ·  Alisa Amouage

There is a moment — usually within the first 30 seconds of a live performance — when you can feel the entire room shift.

Conversations soften. People look up from their phones. The background chatter that filled the space a moment ago fades, not because anyone asked for silence, but because something more compelling has entered the room.

I have experienced this hundreds of times from the stage. And every time, it reminds me why live music is not just entertainment — it is one of the most powerful tools an event can have.

Recorded Music Fills Space. Live Music Transforms It.

There is nothing wrong with a great playlist. But recorded music, no matter how carefully curated, is fundamentally fixed. It was made somewhere else, at another time, for no one in particular. It occupies the air without engaging it.

Live music is different. It is happening now, in this room, for these people. A live performer is reading the audience, adjusting tone and tempo in real time, responding to the energy in the space. That responsiveness creates something a playlist never can: a genuine sense of occasion.

When people feel that something is being performed for them, they become participants rather than bystanders. The entire dynamic of the room changes.

The Science Behind the Shift

This is not just intuition — there is real psychology behind it. Live music triggers the release of oxytocin, the bonding hormone, in ways that recorded music does not replicate to the same degree. When people hear a voice or an instrument in the same physical space, their nervous systems synchronise in subtle ways. Heart rates align. Breathing slows. A shared experience forms, even between strangers.

Event planners know this instinctively, even if they have never read the research. The rooms that people remember are almost always the ones where something live happened.

What I See From the Stage

I have performed at whisky bars, corporate galas, wedding receptions, leadership dinners, and installation ceremonies. The venues are different. The guests are different. But the shift is always the same.

A table of executives who arrived stressed from a long day begin to relax their shoulders by the second song. A room of strangers at a networking event find a natural opening to speak to each other — the music gave them a shared reference point. Guests at a gala dinner linger longer than they planned because leaving now would mean leaving something that feels alive.

Live music creates permission — permission to unwind, to connect, to be present in a way that a corporate agenda rarely allows.

For Event Hosts: What This Means Practically

If you are planning an event and deciding whether live music is worth the investment, here is what I would tell you:

The question is not whether your guests will notice the difference. They will. The question is what you want them to feel — and what you want them to remember about the evening you created.

Live music is not background. Done well, it is the emotional architecture of your event. It sets the tone before a single speech is made. It carries the room through transitions. It gives your guests something to talk about on the way home.

In fifteen years of performing, I have never had a client tell me the live music was too much. I have had many tell me it was the thing that made the night.
